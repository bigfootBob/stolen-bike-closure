Craigslist Paranoia

Understanding the compulsive need to refresh local classifieds at 3 AM looking for a 2018 Trek Marlin 5 that "kinda looks like yours even though the picture is blurry."

Issued by: The Coalition for Refined Searching Algorithms (CORSA) Published: March 29, 2026  |  3rd Edition (Substantially Expanded)
⚠ Coalition Advisory If you are currently reading this between the hours of 11 PM and 4 AM, you may already be experiencing the condition described herein. CORSA recommends you finish reading, then close all tabs. You will not close all tabs. We understand. Please continue.

Executive Summary

Each year, millions of bicycles are stolen. Each year, the owners of those bicycles spend a cumulative estimated 14.7 million hours refreshing Craigslist, Facebook Marketplace, and OfferUp in a state of exhausted, electric-eyed vigilance that clinicians have begun calling Classifieds Paranoia Disorder, and that the rest of us have been calling "Tuesday night" for years.

This document, produced by the Coalition for Refined Searching Algorithms, represents our most comprehensive overview to date of the behavioral, psychological, and logistical dimensions of post-theft marketplace monitoring. It is not a cure. We do not have a cure. We have, however, developed a very precise vocabulary for the problem, which is something.

Phase I: The Rational Search (Hours 0–48)

In the immediate aftermath of a theft, classifieds monitoring begins sensibly. The rider files a police report, notes the serial number, photographs the receipt. They open Craigslist and type in the make, model, and year with focused calm. This phase is characterized by a reasonable conviction that the bike will simply be there, posted by some blissfully unaware fence who didn't notice the personalized handlebar tape.

Searches during Phase I are efficient and well-structured. The subject uses correct terminology. Results are reviewed systematically. The subject closes the laptop at a normal hour and expresses cautious optimism. This is the last time any of this is true.

"The rational search is the bicycle theft experience's version of denial. It is comfortable, organized, and has approximately a 4% success rate. We at CORSA cherish it while it lasts."
— CORSA Field Notes, Vol. 12

Phase II: Scope Expansion (Days 3–10)

By day three, the subject has accepted that their exact bike is not listed under "bikes — by owner." They have begun searching adjacent categories. Then adjacent cities. They are now browsing "general > free stuff" in a metro area four hours away "just to see." Their search terms have evolved.

Day 1

"Trek Marlin 5 2018 blue"

Day 3

"Trek Marlin mountain bike" — city + three surrounding regions

Day 5

"Trek mountain bike" — all sizes, all years, all colors, all conditions

Day 7

"mountain bike" — 83 results reviewed, two flagged as "suspicious," zero confirmed

Day 9

"bike" — 214 results. Subject notes the Huffy in listing #7 has the same stem length. Subject is aware this is meaningless. Subject bookmarks the listing anyway.

Day 11

"blue" — we do not discuss what happened on day 11

Phase III: The 3 AM Threshold

The most clinically significant phase of Craigslist Paranoia is defined not by search terms but by timestamp. CORSA data shows a sharp inflection in session frequency and duration between the hours of 11 PM and 4 AM, peaking at approximately 2:40 AM — a phenomenon the research team calls the Witching Refresh.

Several factors converge to make late-night searching uniquely irrational and uniquely compelling. The subject is tired and therefore less resistant to wishful interpretation. Screen brightness in a dark room creates a false sense of intimacy with the results. And there is, beneath everything, a persistent magical belief that thieves post at night — that somewhere, at this very moment, the person who took the bike is photographing it under a bare bulb and typing a description while you, vigilant and sleepless, wait to catch them.

This belief is not supported by data. CORSA has reviewed posting timestamps for 22,000 used-bike listings across 18 cities. The modal posting time is 4:30 PM on a Saturday. Nobody is posting at 2:40 AM. Nobody except the subject, who is posting a detailed "stolen bike — please look out" notice they have already posted four times this week.

The Blurry Photo Problem

No discussion of Craigslist Paranoia is complete without addressing the Blurry Photo. CORSA analysis of 4,400 used-bike listings found that 34% contained at least one image of insufficient resolution to identify the make, model, or even color of the bicycle with confidence. Of these, 31% received disproportionate engagement from users matching the stolen-bike profile — suggesting that ambiguity, rather than deterring investigation, actively fuels it.

The blurry photo is, for the paranoid searcher, a Rorschach test with a price tag. Where others see an underfocused JPEG of an unknown object, the subject sees outlines, proportions, a particular quality of light. They see what might be the scuff mark on the top tube from that time they leaned it against the parking meter outside the Thai place. They zoom in. The pixels are uncooperative. They zoom in further. They are now examining individual compression artifacts for evidence.

This will not be the last blurry photo they examine tonight.

Treatment & Coalition Recommendations

CORSA does not recommend cold-turkey cessation of classifieds monitoring, as field reports indicate this produces heightened anxiety and a belief that one is "missing the listing" during the abstention period. Instead, we propose a structured harm-reduction approach:

The Two-Tab Rule. At any given time, no more than two bike listings may be open simultaneously. Tabs in excess of two must be closed before a new one is opened. The Coalition acknowledges this rule has a 0% compliance rate but maintains it as a theoretical benchmark.

The Escalation Pause. Before messaging any seller, the subject must wait 20 minutes. This window exists to allow the rational mind to consider whether the 2007 Schwinn they are about to accuse someone of stealing is actually a 2018 Trek. In practice, subjects use the 20 minutes to gather additional evidence and compose a longer message.

The Seller-Benefit Protocol. When a listing appears suspicious, the subject should systematically consider benign explanations before proceeding. The bike is legitimately for sale. The seller is new to the platform. The low price reflects normal used-goods negotiation dynamics. The Coalition has found this exercise is ineffective but time-consuming, which achieves a similar result.

The 4 AM Hard Stop. Devices should be placed in a separate room at 4 AM. Any listing that genuinely contains the stolen bike will still contain it in the morning. The thief is not monitoring refresh timestamps. The deal is not expiring. The bike will still be there at 8 AM, and so will the bad decision you were about to make by contacting that seller with a blurry photo of someone's perfectly normal bicycle.3

Conclusion

Craigslist Paranoia is not irrational. It is the product of genuine loss, genuine hope, and a classifieds marketplace structurally optimized to reward exactly the kind of late-night, low-probability searching that stolen-bike owners are most prone to. The listings are always refreshing. The categories are always adjacent. The photos are always blurry enough.

The Coalition for Refined Searching Algorithms was founded on the belief that better search tools, clearer listing standards, and serial number verification requirements would meaningfully reduce post-theft anguish. We continue to believe this. We also continue to check Craigslist at 2 AM. These things are not mutually exclusive.

If your bike is out there — and it might be — we hope you find it. If it isn't, we hope you eventually close the tab. We'll be here either way, refreshing alongside you.
