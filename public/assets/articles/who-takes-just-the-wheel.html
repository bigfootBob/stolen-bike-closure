The Review of Post-Rational Loss Experience · Vol. IV · Issue 2

Coping With
"Who Takes Just the Wheel?"

An exploration into the deep, unresolved existential dread when you return to the rack to find your frame, one wheel, and absolutely no logic behind what just occurred.

Authored by Prof. Vera Sprocket, MPhil · Department of Inexplicable Loss, Brokenheim University
Peer reviewed by: two colleagues who also had their wheels stolen, separately, which the editorial board considers a conflict of interest and a tragedy
"The frame was still there. The lock was still there. One wheel was still there.
And yet. And yet." — Survivor testimony, Rack B, Maple Street Parking Structure, 2025

I. The Scene

You round the corner and you see it. Your brain, operating in its usual optimistic pre-confirmation mode, registers the general silhouette and begins preparing the muscle memory for mounting. Then something resolves incorrectly. There is a shape where your bike should be, but the shape is wrong. It is too flat. It is listing to one side. It is resting, in a manner that communicates infinite defeat, directly on its exposed fork.

Your bicycle is mostly there. This is, somehow, worse than if it weren't.

The frame: present. The handlebars: present. The rear wheel: present, still locked, faithfully completing its one assigned job while the situation around it deteriorated completely. The front wheel: absent. Gone. Taken. By someone who looked at a complete bicycle locked to a rack and thought — wheel. Just the one wheel. That one, specifically. The front one.

You stand there for a longer time than you would like to admit. You are waiting for your brain to generate an explanation. Your brain is not generating an explanation. Your brain has submitted a ticket and is waiting for the appropriate department to respond.

II. The Philosophical Problem

The full-bike theft, while devastating, is at least coherent. Someone wanted a bicycle. They took the bicycle. The universe subtracted one bicycle from your possession and added it, presumably, to someone else's. Grief is appropriate. The event, however, obeys a certain internal logic that the mind can eventually process and file.

The partial theft does not file.

The partial theft sits in your mental inbox, refusing to be categorized, because it raises a question that philosophy has not adequately addressed: what does it mean for something to be almost stolen? You still have a bicycle. You have most of a bicycle. You have a bicycle in the same way that a chair with one leg is still, technically, furniture. The possession is present. The utility is gone. You have been given a koan shaped like a Trek.

"Is a bicycle without a front wheel still a bicycle? And if it is, why does looking at it feel like looking at a crime scene?"
— Prof. V. Sprocket, office whiteboard, erased and rewritten fourteen times

The Western philosophical tradition has, historically, concerned itself with questions of identity, continuity, and the ship of Theseus. None of these frameworks were developed with quick-release axles in mind, and it shows.

Framework Application to the Partial Theft Verdict
Ship of Theseus If you replace the stolen wheel, is it still your bike? If you don't, is it still a bike? Unhelpfully circular. Provides no guidance on whether to call the insurance company.
Sartrean Existentialism Existence precedes essence; the bike exists but has lost its cycling essence. You are condemned to be free — and also to walk. Technically accurate. Emotionally useless.
Camus & Absurdism The universe is indifferent. The theft is without meaning. You must imagine Sisyphus happy, but Sisyphus did not have to carry his boulder 1.4 miles to the train station. Points for honesty. Zero practical utility.
Stoicism The wheel was never truly yours. Distinguish what is and isn't in your control. The wheel is gone; your response is not. Valid in theory. Very easy to say when your bike has both wheels.
Pragmatism A belief is true insofar as it works. The belief that your bike is "fine" does not work. Therefore: false. The most useful framework so far. Still doesn't get you home.

III. The Psychological Stages of Partial Theft Processing

Standard grief literature describes five stages: denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance. The partial theft generates six stages, with one unique to this experience that does not appear in Kübler-Ross and which we have designated Prolonged Confusion.

1
Inventory Disbelief

The subject counts the wheels multiple times. They crouch. They peer. They walk around the bike to confirm from a different angle. They are aware they are doing this. They do it again anyway. The wheel count remains one.

2
Prolonged Confusion (Novel Stage)

The subject understands that a theft has occurred. They do not understand why this theft. This stage can last minutes or days. The subject replays the logic, searching for an entry point. They consider whether they are somehow in the wrong location. They check if there's another rack. This is the stage this paper is primarily about.

3
Misplaced Gratitude

The subject notices the frame is still there and feels, briefly, relieved. Then feels immediately angry at themselves for feeling relieved. Then acknowledges that, okay, the frame is worth more than the wheel, so technically—. They stop that thought before it finishes.

4
Forensic Speculation

The subject begins reconstructing the scene. The thief had a quick-release tool — or didn't need one, if the subject used a quick-release. Did they plan for the front wheel specifically, or was it opportunistic? Was someone else's front wheel already taken, and their bike stripped further, and this is all part of a larger ecosystem the subject is only now glimpsing? They are now thinking about the criminal supply chain. They are still standing at the rack.

5
The Carrier Question

How did they carry it? A single wheel is awkward. You can roll it, but only in a straight line, and you look suspicious. You can carry it under your arm. You can put it in a bag, if the bag is large enough. The subject spends meaningful time thinking about this. The subject's coworkers will later describe them as "distracted" during the 3 PM meeting.

6
Reluctant Pragmatism

The subject determines what to do with the remaining bike. This is a surprisingly difficult decision. They cannot ride it home. They cannot leave it (it will be further stripped). They can carry it, but it is a full bike frame with one wheel and that is not a casual thing to carry on the train. They call someone. They feel embarrassed about why.

"The lock worked perfectly.
This is, somehow, part of the problem."

IV. Theories of Motive

In the absence of a confession — which, in 99.4% of partial-theft cases, is not forthcoming — the subject is left to theorize. This section catalogs the most commonly proposed explanations collected from our subject pool, ranked by internal coherence.

Theory 01 — Highest Coherence The Replacement Parts Hypothesis

The thief needed a specific wheel for a specific bicycle they already owned or had recently stolen. They identified the subject's front wheel as compatible, removed it efficiently, and departed. The act was surgical, targeted, and rational within its own context — a context the subject was not privy to and does not find comforting to imagine.

Theory 02 — Moderate Coherence The Interrupted Theft Hypothesis

The thief intended to take the entire bicycle but was disturbed — by a passing pedestrian, a shop owner, an approaching bus, the sudden and inconvenient arrival of a conscience — and departed with only what they had already detached. This theory offers the subject a narrative of near-rescue that is entirely unsatisfying because the wheel is still gone.

Theory 03 — Low Coherence The Upgrade Hypothesis

The thief had a bicycle they rode to the scene. They preferred the subject's front wheel to their own. They swapped wheels, leaving their old wheel behind — except they didn't, because there was no wheel left at the scene, which undermines this theory considerably. Nevertheless, subjects propose it frequently, perhaps because it implies the thief departed on a degraded bicycle, which is some comfort, however speculative.

Theory 04 — No Coherence The Chaos Agent Hypothesis

There was no plan. There was no need. There was an unattended wheel on a quick-release skewer and a person who, in that moment, simply decided. Perhaps on a whim. Perhaps as a dare. Perhaps because the quick-release is a beautiful design that rewards curiosity in the worst possible way. The wheel came off in seconds. And then they just... left. With it. Because why not.

This theory is the most disturbing because it cannot be argued with.

V. The Lock's Role in This

We must speak about the lock.

The lock is still there. It is attached to the frame, the rear wheel, and the rack, exactly as designed, exactly as the subject installed it that morning with the quiet confidence of someone who has taken a reasonable precaution. The lock did not fail. The lock succeeded completely at the job it was assigned.

This is why looking at the lock feels like a betrayal.

The lock secured what was locked and nothing more. The front wheel, on its quick-release skewer, was not locked. The front wheel was, by the conventions of everyday cycling, considered secure enough by virtue of being attached to a locked bicycle. This is a convention the thief did not share. The thief and the subject had different mental models of what "locked" implied, and the subject is now on the losing side of that philosophical disagreement.2

Several subjects in our study reported finding themselves subsequently overlocking. One locks both wheels, the frame, and the saddle. One has purchased a chain long enough to encircle the entire bike twice. One has begun locking the handlebar grips. When asked if this seemed excessive, they said: "It seemed excessive to take just the wheel."

The researcher had no response to this.

VI. On Coming to Terms

Resolution, in the conventional therapeutic sense, may not be fully available for the partial-theft survivor. The complete theft, however painful, at least produces a clear before-and-after. The partial theft produces something murkier: a damaged object, a logical gap, and a nagging sense that the universe has played a joke that it refuses to explain.

Subjects who report the highest wellbeing scores at the six-month follow-up share one common cognitive adaptation: they have stopped trying to understand why. They have filed the event not under crimes with motives but under things that happened, for which no satisfying explanation exists — alongside sunburns in October, socks disappearing in the wash, and the specific feeling of stepping off an escalator and still lifting your foot for a step that isn't there.

This is not acceptance. It is something adjacent to acceptance. It is the cognitive equivalent of closing a tab you know you'll never find satisfying and opening a new one.

The wheel is gone. The frame remains. You are, in some fundamental sense, still a cyclist — a cyclist currently carrying a partial bicycle on the subway, getting looks, declining to explain.

That is, for now, enough.
